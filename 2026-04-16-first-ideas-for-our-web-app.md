At the start of this project, our group did not decide the final topic immediately. We first talked about a few different ideas because we wanted to choose something that was actually useful, not just something that sounded interesting.

The first idea was a student support website. Since we are students, this idea felt easy to understand. We thought about a website where students could share study tips, assignment reminders, campus information, and maybe advice for new students. This idea was useful, but after talking about it more, I felt it was too similar to Canvas, Ed Discussion, Discord, or WeChat groups. If we built another student platform, users might ask why they need to use it.

The second idea was a local community website. People could share local events, second-hand items, supermarket information, or neighbourhood news. I thought this idea had a lot of possibilities. However, the problem was that it was too broad. It could become a shopping website, an events website, a news website, or even a services platform. Because of that, it was hard for us to decide what the most important function should be.

The third idea was a social website for middle-aged and older adults. This idea came from the thought that many older users may want a simpler and more friendly place to communicate online. They may not want a platform that is too fast, too complicated, or too focused on young people. We thought this website could let them share daily life, talk with others, post useful life tips, and maybe receive supermarket coupons or platform benefits.

At this stage, we had not fully chosen the final idea yet. But I already felt the third idea was more special than the others. It had a clearer target group and it was easier to connect the functions to real daily needs.

My main takeaway from this week is that a web app topic should not only be creative. It also needs a clear user group and a clear reason for existing. Otherwise, the website may become too general and difficult to design.
